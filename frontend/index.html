I'm Karthik — based in the UK, originally from somewhere far more colourful, and permanently restless. I don't travel to escape life. I travel because the world outside the familiar is where I feel most alive.

Entry 01  ·  Scotland  ·  Spring

Where the Sea Has
No Patience
for Tourists

St. Abbs Head, Scottish Borders  ·  1 hour from Edinburgh

There are places that don't care whether you visit or not. St. Abbs is one of them. Tucked into the southeastern edge of Scotland, it sits there — ancient, windswept, completely indifferent to the fact that most people have never heard of it.

"The cliffs don't pose. The sea doesn't perform. It all just exists — with a quiet violence that makes the rest of the world feel very far away."

The rocks are rust-coloured and enormous, pushed from the earth so long ago they've forgotten they ever moved. Seabirds claim every ledge with total confidence nothing will disturb them.

What surprised me most were the wildflowers. Right at the cliff edge, clusters of sea thrift — soft purple-pink and completely unbothered by the wind. I stayed longer than I planned. That seems to happen in places that don't rush you.

Entry 02  ·  Ireland  ·  Winter — Spring

The Island That
Refuses to Let You Go

Dublin · Carrick-a-Rede · Slieve League · The Dark Hedges

Ireland doesn't ease you in. From the moment you arrive, it is immediately, unapologetically itself — loud and quiet at the same time, ancient and alive, the kind of place that makes you feel strangely emotional about things you cannot fully explain.

"Standing on that rope bridge — the Atlantic crashing below, the whole structure swaying — I felt something between absolute terror and absolute freedom."

The Carrick-a-Rede Rope Bridge at dusk is one of those experiences no photograph captures. Golden light bleeding behind cliffs. The sound of the sea below, indifferent and vast. You walk across and something shifts permanently.

Then the Dark Hedges — ancient beeches on a quiet country road, branches tangled like something written before electricity. Dublin from above: bridge after bridge, golden at dusk. Beautiful in a way that sneaks up on you.

Entry 03  ·  Scotland  ·  All seasons

A City That Wears
Its History Like
A Coat It Never Takes Off

Edinburgh · Castle · Dean Village · Princes Street · Scott Monument

Edinburgh makes you feel like you arrived in the middle of a very long story — one that started long before you and will continue long after. The stones are too dark, too old, too heavy with meaning for it to feel any other way.

"A rainbow broke over Princes Street while I stood in the rain — and every single person on that street stopped walking at exactly the same moment."

The castle watches the city with the patience of something that has seen several centuries come and go. The Scott Monument glows amber at dusk — Gothic, enormous, gloriously unnecessary.

Dean Village is hidden at the bottom of a gorge, ten minutes from Princes Street. Old mill buildings beside a rushing river, moss on every stone. Edinburgh hides things like this around corners, to reward those who slow down enough to look.

Entry 04  ·  England  ·  Road trip

The Roads That
Take You There Are
Half the Story

Leicester, England  ·  Via the Pennines & rolling moorland

There is a particular kind of peace that comes from being in a moving car with a good road ahead and hills on either side. Britain does this better than almost anywhere — within an hour of any city, the motorway opens into something that looks nothing like England is supposed to look.

"The hills were lit gold on one side, deep green on the other. The road curved through them like it was showing off. I didn't want it to end."

Leicester itself is quietly multicultural, genuinely warm, and more interesting than most people who haven't been there expect. But on this journey, the drive was the destination — moorland catching the last of the afternoon light, valleys that had no business being that beautiful on a weekday.

Some of the best travel happens between the places you planned to visit. You just have to be paying attention when it does.

Entry 05  ·  England  ·  Jurassic Coast

England Has a
Wild Side and
Dorset Is It

Durdle Door · Jurassic Coast · Dorset, England

Most people think of England as polite, managed, knowable. Then they go to Dorset and reconsider. The Jurassic Coast is 185 million years old and it shows — white chalk cliffs dropping sheer into churning water, waves crashing with total indifference.

"Durdle Door is one of those shapes that doesn't seem possible until you're standing in front of it. A natural limestone arch worn through by the sea over centuries. Quietly magnificent."

The sea shifts between turquoise and deep navy depending on depth and light. I stood on the clifftop above Durdle Door and felt, very briefly, like the world was exactly the right size. England does that to you sometimes, when you're not expecting it.

Entry 06  ·  England  ·  Atlantic Coast

The Rainbow
Landed Right
Where It Should

Cornwall, England  ·  Atlantic Coast

Cornwall feels like it belongs to a different country entirely. There is something in the light here — the way it falls on the cliffs, the way the Atlantic catches it and throws it back — that feels more ancient than modern.

"A rainbow came in from the sea while I was standing on the clifftop and touched down on the rock below me. I stayed completely still, as if moving might make it disappear."

The cliffs are layered and dramatic — stratified rock worn into shelves and hollows by water that has been at this longer than anything in England's written history. The sea below is a colour that shouldn't exist in this part of the world: genuinely turquoise, genuinely clear.

Cornwall has the quality of somewhere that knows it is beautiful but doesn't feel the need to perform it. It just is. That is rarer than it sounds.

Entry 07  ·  Scottish Highlands  ·  Autumn

Where Scotland
Becomes Almost
Too Much to Bear

Glenfinnan Viaduct · Glencoe · Scottish Highlands

There is a version of Scotland that exists in the imagination — enormous mountains wreathed in cloud, a lone white cottage in a valley so vast it makes you feel like a small and temporary thing. I had always assumed it was slightly exaggerated. Then I drove through Glencoe and understood that if anything, the imagination undersells it.

"A single white cottage at the foot of a mountain that had disappeared entirely into cloud. One tree beside it. A path leading nowhere visible. It looked like the last place on earth — and somehow exactly the right one."

The Glenfinnan Viaduct is something else entirely. Twenty-one arches curving through green Highland hills, carrying a railway line that has no business being this beautiful. You know it from a thousand photographs and films, and you think familiarity might dull it. It doesn't. Standing below those arches with the hills rising behind them, it is simply one of the most quietly magnificent things Britain has built.

Scotland in the Highlands doesn't perform. It simply exists on a scale that makes everything else feel small — and somehow, extraordinarily, that makes you feel more alive rather than less.

Experiences

What travel means to Karthik
I.

Scotland on a scale that humbles you

A lone white cottage in Glencoe beneath a mountain that had vanished into cloud. The Glenfinnan Viaduct curving through hills that shouldn't exist outside a film. Scotland in the Highlands doesn't perform. It simply exists — and that is enough to change you.

II.

The moment you decide to cross

The Carrick-a-Rede rope bridge taught me something permanent. There is always a moment before you step onto something uncertain. That moment — the one before you decide to go — is where the real journey begins every single time.

III.

Rainbows that choose their landing spots well

Edinburgh's rainbow stopped a whole street. Cornwall's touched down on the clifftop below where I was standing. There is a particular kind of moment where the light does something so unexpected and so perfect that the only right response is to stand completely still and not waste it.

IV.

The road is always part of the destination

The drive to Leicester through golden Pennine moorland. The approach to Glencoe through valleys that grow larger and quieter with every mile. The best British travel often happens in the space between the places you planned — you just have to be paying attention.
